# Luma Client Personas

## Persona 1: The Operator — "Sarah the OBM"

### Demographics & Background

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Name** | Sarah Chen |
| **Age** | 34 |
| **Role** | Online Business Manager (OBM) / Fractional COO |
| **Business** | Solo service provider, registered LLC, 2.5 years in business |
| **Clients** | 8 active retainer clients (sweet spot of 3-15 range) |
| **Revenue** | $8K-$14K/month from retainers ($1K-$1.8K per client avg) |
| **Location** | Works remotely from home office, US-based |
| **Education** | Business degree, self-taught on PM tools and automations |
| **Work hours** | 35-45 hours/week, flexes around client time zones |

### Technical Proficiency

- **Comfortable** with SaaS tools: ClickUp, Asana, Google Workspace, Slack, Zoom, GoHighLevel
- **Can navigate** basic automations (Zapier, GHL workflows) but not a developer
- **Learns new tools** through YouTube tutorials and peer communities, not documentation
- **Browser-based** workflow — uses Chrome with 20+ tabs open, rarely uses desktop apps
- **Mobile** for Slack/email triage only; does real work on laptop
- **Expects** tools to work on first try — if onboarding takes more than 10 minutes, she'll abandon and revisit "when I have time" (which means never)

### Goals & Motivations

1. **Prove her value without self-promotion.** Sarah does excellent work, but it's invisible. Clients only notice when something breaks, not when 47 things ran smoothly. She wants the work to speak for itself through a clear record.

2. **Protect her scope and sanity.** Scope creep is her #1 revenue leak. Clients ask for "one more thing" weekly. She needs a paper trail that makes scope boundaries visible without making the conversation adversarial.

3. **Retain clients longer.** Losing a $1,500/month client costs her 2-3 months of pipeline effort to replace. If clients can see the value they're getting, they're less likely to churn or question retainer fees.

4. **Reclaim reporting time.** She currently spends ~2 hours per client per month building manual reports in Notion + Loom. That's 16 hours/month — essentially losing a full client's worth of billable capacity to admin work.

5. **Grow without hiring.** She wants to take on 2-3 more clients without working more hours. Reducing admin overhead is the only way to create that capacity.

### Pain Points & Frustrations

| Pain Point | Severity | Current Workaround |
|-----------|----------|-------------------|
| "What have you been doing?" conversations | **Critical** — triggers self-doubt and resentment | Reactive Loom walkthroughs, ad-hoc Slack recaps |
| Manual monthly reporting | **High** — 16 hrs/month of unbillable work | Notion templates copied monthly, screenshots of ClickUp, assembled by hand |
| Scope creep with no paper trail | **High** — loses $500-$2K/month in untracked work | Tries to push back verbally on calls, often caves to avoid conflict |
| Work fragmented across 5+ tools | **Medium** — no single view of what she delivered | Keeps a personal Google Sheet as a "did list" |
| Can't articulate impact at renewal time | **Medium** — renewals feel like a negotiation, not a celebration | Scrambles to pull metrics from various dashboards before renewal call |
| Fear of adopting "another tool" | **Medium** — tool fatigue is real, last 3 tools she tried are collecting dust | Only commits to tools recommended by her OBM peer group |

### Usage Patterns & Frequency

| Activity | Frequency | Duration | Device |
|----------|-----------|----------|--------|
| Log delivery items | 3-5x per week | 2-5 minutes per session | Desktop (laptop) |
| Review scope tracker | 2-3x per week | 1-2 minutes | Desktop |
| Generate/share client view | 1-2x per month per client | 5-10 minutes | Desktop |
| Set up new client | ~1x per month | 10-15 minutes | Desktop |
| Review capacity dashboard | 1x per week | 2-3 minutes | Desktop |
| Handle scope request | 1-3x per week | 3-5 minutes | Desktop |

**Session context:** Sarah logs into Luma during her "admin block" — usually Monday mornings (planning week) and Friday afternoons (wrapping up). She might also log a delivery item right after finishing a task, while it's fresh. She does NOT want to context-switch into Luma during deep work.

### Jobs-to-be-Done

#### Primary Job
> **When** I finish a deliverable or complete a chunk of client work, **I want to** log it in under 30 seconds with minimal friction, **so I can** build an automatic record of value delivered without disrupting my workflow.

#### Supporting Jobs

| Job Statement | Priority | Current Satisfaction |
|--------------|----------|---------------------|
| When a client asks "what have you been doing?", I want to share one link that shows everything, so I can redirect the conversation from doubt to data. | **P0** | Unmet — no tool does this |
| When a client asks for something outside scope, I want to flag it with one click and show them their scope usage, so I can protect boundaries without being confrontational. | **P0** | Unmet — relies on verbal pushback |
| When it's end of month, I want a client-ready summary generated automatically, so I can reclaim 2 hours per client of reporting time. | **P0** | Poorly met — manual Notion + Loom |
| When I'm considering taking on a new client, I want to see my capacity across all clients at a glance, so I can make the decision without guessing. | **P1** | Poorly met — mental math + gut feel |
| When it's renewal time, I want to show 3-6 months of delivery history with impact, so I can justify my retainer fee with evidence. | **P1** | Unmet — scrambles for data |
| When I set up a new client, I want to use a template that matches common service packages, so I can start tracking from day one in under 5 minutes. | **P1** | N/A — no tool to set up |

### Success Criteria

Sarah considers Luma successful if:

1. **Time saved:** Monthly client reporting drops from ~2 hours/client to <15 minutes/client (80% reduction)
2. **Scope protection:** She has a documented record when scope discussions happen — she can point to data, not memory
3. **Client confidence:** At least 3 of her 8 clients open their Luma view unprompted (meaning they self-serve instead of asking)
4. **Zero "what are you doing?" conversations** — clients already know because the view tells them
5. **Adoption sticks:** She uses Luma for more than 30 days (her personal threshold for any tool earning its place)
6. **Worth paying for:** She'd pay $29-49/month without hesitation because it saves her 16+ hours/month of admin work

### Behavioral Traits

- **Batch processor.** Doesn't want real-time notifications. Wants to log work in batches during admin blocks.
- **Visual thinker.** Responds to progress bars, charts, and status colors. Walls of text lose her.
- **Community-driven adopter.** Will try Luma if her OBM Facebook group recommends it. Word-of-mouth is her discovery channel.
- **Risk-averse with clients.** Will NOT share a tool with clients unless she's 100% confident it looks professional and works flawlessly. One broken link = she stops using it.
- **Data-minimal.** Wants to enter the least possible information for the maximum output. If a field is optional, she'll skip it.

## Persona 2: The Client — "Marcus the Founder"

### Demographics & Background

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Name** | Marcus Williams |
| **Age** | 41 |
| **Role** | Founder & CEO of a DTC e-commerce brand |
| **Company** | 12-person team, $1.5M-$3M annual revenue |
| **Operator relationship** | Pays Sarah $1,500/month retainer for operations management |
| **Location** | US-based, travels frequently for trade shows and partnerships |
| **Education** | MBA, strong on strategy and sales, delegates operations |
| **Work style** | High-level thinker, low patience for details, makes fast decisions |

### Technical Proficiency

- **Uses daily:** Email (Gmail), Slack, maybe a CRM he barely touches
- **Delegates** all tool management to his operator or team — he doesn't set up accounts, configure dashboards, or learn new interfaces
- **Mobile-first** for consuming information — checks things between meetings, in Ubers, at airports
- **Zero tolerance** for friction — if something requires a login, he'll ask his assistant to "just check it for me"
- **Understands** business metrics (revenue, CAC, LTV) but not project management concepts (sprints, backlogs, scope allocation)
- **Will not** download an app, create an account, or remember a password for a tool that isn't core to his business

### Goals & Motivations

1. **Know his money is well-spent.** He pays $1,500/month and sometimes wonders what he's getting. Not because the work isn't happening, but because he can't see it. He wants confidence, not control.

2. **Stay informed without meetings.** Every status update meeting is 30 minutes he could spend on sales or strategy. He wants to self-serve the answer to "how's everything going?" without scheduling a call.

3. **Make fast decisions.** When something needs his approval, he wants to see it, understand it in 10 seconds, and tap "approved" or "needs changes." No back-and-forth email threads.

4. **Understand scope boundaries.** He's vaguely aware his retainer covers "20 hours" but has no idea how much is used. When he asks for extra things, he doesn't realize he's creating scope creep — he'd respect boundaries if they were visible.

5. **Feel like a priority.** He's paying premium rates and wants to feel like his operator is on top of things. A professional, organized delivery view signals competence and care.

### Pain Points & Frustrations

| Pain Point | Severity | Current Experience |
|-----------|----------|-------------------|
| "I have no idea what my operator does all month" | **Critical** — creates distrust, even when work is excellent | Gets a Loom video he doesn't have time to watch, or a Notion page he forgets to check |
| Too many tools to check | **High** — "I'm not logging into ClickUp to see what's done" | Asks operator on Slack: "Hey, where are we on X?" — wastes both their time |
| Approval bottlenecks | **Medium** — things sit in his inbox, he doesn't see them as urgent | Operator sends Slack DMs, emails, follow-ups — Marcus eventually responds days later |
| No sense of scope | **Medium** — unknowingly requests out-of-scope work | Surprised when operator brings up "extra hours" — "I thought that was included" |
| Status meetings feel wasteful | **Medium** — 30 minutes to hear what could be a 2-minute read | Attends half-heartedly, multitasks during calls |

### Usage Patterns & Frequency

| Activity | Frequency | Duration | Device |
|----------|-----------|----------|--------|
| Open client view link | 1-3x per month | 30 seconds - 2 minutes | **Mobile** (80%) / Desktop (20%) |
| Review monthly summary | 1x per month | 1-2 minutes | Mobile or desktop |
| Approve/request revision on deliverables | As needed (2-5x/month) | 10-30 seconds per item | Mobile |
| Check scope usage | Rarely (maybe 1x/month, if visible) | 10-20 seconds | Whatever device is handy |
| Review past months | At renewal time (~quarterly) | 3-5 minutes | Desktop |

**Session context:** Marcus opens the Luma link when his operator shares it (usually via Slack or email DM). He might also bookmark it and check it when he's wondering "what am I paying for?" — typically during a commute, waiting for a meeting, or during a quarterly budget review. He will NEVER proactively seek out this information unless it's one tap away.

### Jobs-to-be-Done

#### Primary Job
> **When** I'm wondering what my operator has been doing or whether my retainer is worth it, **I want to** open one link and immediately see a clear summary of what's been delivered, **so I can** feel confident my investment is working without scheduling a meeting or watching a video.

#### Supporting Jobs

| Job Statement | Priority | Current Satisfaction |
|--------------|----------|---------------------|
| When my operator delivers something that needs my sign-off, I want to approve it in one tap from my phone, so I can keep things moving without email threads. | **P0** | Unmet — approvals happen in Slack/email chaos |
| When I open the link, I want to understand everything within 10 seconds, so I can get back to running my business. | **P0** | Unmet — current reports are too dense or require context |
| When I'm reviewing my budget, I want to see how my scope is being used (X of Y hours/deliverables), so I can understand the value per dollar without asking. | **P1** | Unmet — no visibility into scope usage |
| When it's time to renew my operator's retainer, I want to look back at months of delivery history, so I can make the renewal decision based on evidence. | **P1** | Poorly met — relies on memory and gut feeling |
| When I want something done that might be outside scope, I want to see clearly that it's extra, so I can make an informed request without awkward conversations later. | **P2** | Unmet — scope is invisible until it becomes a conflict |

### Success Criteria

Marcus considers the client view successful if:

1. **Instant clarity:** He opens the link and understands the status in under 10 seconds — no scrolling, no clicking, no learning curve
2. **No login, no account, no app:** He taps a link and it works. Period. If it asks him to create an account, he's out.
3. **Mobile-friendly:** It looks good and works on his iPhone. No pinching, no horizontal scrolling, no broken layouts.
4. **Feels professional:** The view looks polished — like something a premium service provider would use. It reinforces that his operator is organized and competent.
5. **Replaces status meetings:** He can cancel (or shorten) monthly check-ins because the view gives him what he needs
6. **Approvals are effortless:** Tap "Approve" and it's done. No forms, no comments required (optional is fine).

### Behavioral Traits

- **Scanner, not reader.** Looks at headlines, numbers, and colors. Skips paragraphs. Design must communicate through visual hierarchy, not text density.
- **Mobile-first information consumer.** Checks things between meetings, in transit, in bed. If it doesn't work on a phone screen, it doesn't exist.
- **Impatient but not dismissive.** He values his operator and wants the relationship to work — he just won't invest effort to make a tool work. The tool must meet him where he is.
- **Trusts visuals over words.** A progress bar at 75% communicates more than "15 of 20 hours used." A green checkmark communicates more than "Completed on Feb 12."
- **Decision-maker, not collaborator.** He wants to approve/reject, not discuss. Give him clear options and he'll decide quickly.
- **Bookmark-and-forget.** If he bookmarks the link once, he might check it monthly. If he has to find it again, he won't.

## Design Implications

### From Sarah's Persona

1. **Delivery logging must be <30 seconds.** Minimal required fields. Smart defaults. Keyboard shortcuts for power users.
2. **Scope tracker must be visually prominent.** Sarah checks it 2-3x/week — it should be above the fold on every client view.
3. **Client view must look flawless before she shares it.** Preview mode is essential. She won't share until she's confident.
4. **Templates accelerate adoption.** Pre-built scope types (OBM retainer, VA package) reduce setup friction.
5. **Batch-friendly design.** Let her log multiple items, review scope across clients, and generate links in one admin session.

### From Marcus's Persona

1. **Client view is a single-scroll page, not a dashboard.** Summary at top, details below. No tabs, no navigation, no learning curve.
2. **Mobile-first responsive design is non-negotiable.** 80% of Marcus's views are on phone. Design for 375px width first.
3. **Visual hierarchy over text.** Progress rings > paragraph descriptions. Status colors > status labels. Numbers > sentences.
4. **Approval actions must be thumb-friendly.** Large tap targets, clear labels, optional (not required) comment field.
5. **Zero onboarding.** The page must be self-explanatory. If it needs a tutorial, it's too complex.

### Shared Implications

1. **The client view is the product's marketing.** If Marcus loves it, he'll tell other founders. If founders talk, their operators discover Luma. The client experience drives operator acquisition.
2. **Scope visibility benefits both sides.** Sarah gets protection; Marcus gets transparency. Design scope as a shared artifact, not an adversarial tool.
3. **Professionalism is a feature.** Both personas care about polish — Sarah because it reflects on her brand, Marcus because it signals competence. Generic-looking UI will hurt adoption.
